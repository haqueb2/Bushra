McMaster University
BIOCHEM 3R06 Research Project

McMaster Department of Pathology and Molecular Medicine, Hamilton ON

During the 2019/2020 Fall and Winter terms, I enrolled in the BIOCHEM 3R06 course where I embarked on an eight-month research project under the supervision by a member of the Department of Pathology and Molecular Medicine. The objective of our research was to analyze data from several sample types (placenta, fibroblast, blood, etc.) of a patient  diagnosed with a rare genetic disorder known as mosaic genome-wide paternal uniparental disomy (mGWpUPD). This analysis was accomplished using genome-wide DNA methylation microarray analysis with the Illumina Infinium MethylationEPIC BeadChip technology, to assess its efficacy in efforts to improve diagnostic methods of mGWpUPD. 

My specific role in this research project was to further characterize molecular etiologies of this disorder through DNA methylation profiling analysis of relevant imprinted gene loci. From this, I was able to describe the percent mosaicism of various tissues and compare our findings to 18 earlier reported patients to understand its linkage to clinical phenotypes. As such, I wanted to use the analysis to understand the anticipated clinical symptoms of our patient and outline a precedent treatment plan. This research project was an opportunity to immerse myself into the scientific process and develop skills and techniques in bioinformatics and statistics. In a broader view, my research project was a step into raising awareness to rare disorders, which often lacks motivation in research and investigation within the scientific community. 

Sept 2018 - April 2019

McMaster University
BIOCHEM 2L06 Research Project

McMaster University, Hamilton ON

In the BIOCHEM 2L06 research project, our goal was to perform a series of experiments pertaining to molecular cloning of the folA gene followed by expression, lysis and purification of the resulting DHFR protein. DHFR is an enzyme that catalyzes the reduction of DHF to THF, which is a molecule playing a role in the biosynthesis of molecules including purines, thymidine and methionine. Since it plays an essential role in the cell, it is a common target for antibiotics such as TMP. In this project, the folA-pET26b plasmid was transformed into E. coli BL21 (DE3), for expression, purification and crystallization of DHFR. Ultimately, future efforts may result in use of protein crystals for structure determination with X-ray crystallography. This will enable further understanding of structure-function relationships of DHFR to produce novel antibiotics. Overall, the project serve as the preliminary steps of the drug discovery process prior to development, pre-clinical and clinical studies of potential antibiotics for DHFR as a target for E. coli cell inhibition. 

This year-long research project provided exposure to various laboratory techniques including: PCR, primer design, restriction enzyme digestion reactions, agarose gel electrophoresis, heat shock transformation, Bradford assay, SDS-PAGE, sitting drop protein crystallization.

Sept 2018 - Oct 2018

McMaster University
Myzus Persicae EβF Response as an Alternative Pest Control Measure

McMaster University, Hamilton ON

In this 6-week project, my group members and I studied parasitism of Myzus persicae, which poses one of the greatest agricultural and horticultural threats due to its ability to significantly reduce crop yields. However, insecticides, the most common pest control measure, pose environmental and pest resistance concerns. The alarm pheromone (E)-β-farnesene (EβF), released by M. persicae in response to predators, was explored in our study in order to assess its effect during predator introduction as a method of pest control. Although the ‘primary effect’ of predators is the consumption of M. persicae, the ‘secondary effect’, defined as the release of EβF, may greatly aid in pest control since it is known to elicit dropping and avoidance behaviour in M. persicae.

The location of M. persicae on its host plant, Arabidopsis thaliana, was observed in this study with varying frequencies of EβF, which is found in chamomile oil. The response behavior was quantified by assessing percent change in number of M. persicae from within the inner 30% radius of the plant– preferred by aphids due to high nutritional content. Our study found no significant difference in location of M. persicae on A. thaliana in response to EβF at varied frequencies of its application. This indicates that the use of predators mainly for the ‘secondary effect’ as a pest control measure may be ineffective. Various factors including aphid distribution, physical stimuli, odour-masking chemicals and age-dependent behaviour may have influenced the outcome of this study.

Feb 2018 - April 2018

McMaster University
A GIS Study: Tasmanian Devil Facial Tumour Disease

McMaster University, Hamilton ON

This study aimed to understand the spread of Devil Facial Tumour Disease (DFTD) in Tasmania to determine how the interactions between Tasmanian devils are influenced by the external factors of topography and urbanization. To examine these factors on a spatial and temporal scale, Geographic Information Systems (ArcGIS) were used with a variety of pre-existing layers as well as created layers. By mapping the DFTD influence throughout Tasmania as well as elevation, fox populations, human populations, and mining sites, several connections were drawn. These influences enhance DFTD rate of spread and drive further extinction from interspecific competition and carcinogen exposure due to urbanization. We suggest that identifying key external factors influencing the spread of DFTD in Tasmanian devils, further action can be taken to prevent the species’ extinction.
